#### EXERCISE 1: FRONT PLATE RAISE

**Primary Muscles:** Deltoideus (anterior head - clavicular portion)
**Secondary Muscles:** Deltoideus (lateral head), Pectoralis Major (clavicular head), Serratus Anterior, Trapezius (upper fibers)
**Type:** Isolation
**Equipment:** Weight Plate
**Level:** Beginner

**Scientific Evidence:**
- Anterior deltoid: 80-100% MVIC activation
- Lateral deltoid: 48% average activation (stabilizer role)
- Frontal raise produces higher EMG activity than lateral raise variations during concentric phase
- Serratus anterior provides scapular stability

**References:**
1. An Electromyographic Analysis of Lateral Raise Variations and Frontal Raise in Competitive Bodybuilders. PubMed. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32824894/
2. Sweeney, S. (2018). Electromyographic analysis of the deltoid muscle during various shoulder exercises. https://minds.wisconsin.edu/handle/1793/70129
3. Surface Electromyographic Analysis of Exercises for the Trapezius and Serratus. JOSPT. https://www.jospt.org/doi/pdf/10.2519/jospt.2003.33.5.247

**Beginner Steps:**
1. Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, holding weight plate with both hands at thigh level
2. Grip plate at 10 o'clock and 2 o'clock positions with palms facing down
3. Engage core and maintain neutral spine with shoulders back
4. Raise plate forward and upward in smooth arc, keeping arms relatively straight
5. Continue lifting until plate reaches eye level or shoulder height
6. Pause briefly at top, then slowly lower back to starting position
7. Complete 10-15 repetitions avoiding momentum

**Advanced Cues:**
1. Maintain scapular retraction to prevent upper trap dominance
2. Lead movement with anterior deltoid, not arms
3. Controlled eccentric (3 seconds) maximizes time under tension
4. Keep plate 8-12 inches in front of face at top for constant tension
5. Avoid hyperextending spine or using lower back momentum

**Sports Applications:**
Boxing, Swimming, Baseball, Tennis, Volleyball, American Football, MMA, Wrestling, Basketball, Gymnastics, Bodybuilding, CrossFit

#### EXERCISE 2: KNEELING SINGLE-ARM CABLE REAR DELT RAISE

**Primary Muscles:** Deltoideus (posterior head)
**Secondary Muscles:** Rhomboideus Major and Minor, Trapezius (middle fibers), Infraspinatus, Teres Minor
**Type:** Isolation
**Equipment:** Cable Machine with D-handle
**Level:** Intermediate

**Scientific Evidence:**
- Seated rear lateral raise and 45-degree incline row produce highest posterior deltoid activation
- Internal rotation and neutral grip positions increase posterior deltoid engagement
- Cable provides constant tension throughout ROM vs free weights
- Kneeling eliminates lower body momentum

**References:**
1. Different Shoulder Exercises Affect the Activation of Deltoid Portions in Resistance-Trained Individuals. PMC. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7706677/
2. Investigation of Deltoid Muscle Activation From Different Angles in Body Building Athletes. PMC. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10600597/
3. Effect of Hand Position on EMG Activity of the Posterior Shoulder Musculature. ResearchGate. https://www.researchgate.net/publication/234098467

**Beginner Steps:**
1. Set cable pulley at shoulder height, attach D-handle, kneel on mat perpendicular to machine
2. Reach across body with arm furthest from machine to grab handle
3. Position body so cable crosses in front of torso, maintain upright kneeling posture
4. Pull handle outward and backward in horizontal arc, leading with elbow
5. Continue until arm is perpendicular to torso or slightly behind body plane
6. Squeeze shoulder blade toward spine at peak, pause 1-2 seconds
7. Return with control, complete all reps one side before switching

**Advanced Cues:**
1. Kneeling eliminates momentum and increases core stabilization demands
2. Visualize pulling elbow backward, not moving hand
3. Slight internal rotation at start increases posterior deltoid activation
4. Maintain constant cable tension - don't allow weight to fully rest
5. Scapular retraction at end range maximizes rhomboid involvement

**Sports Applications:**
Swimming, Baseball, Tennis, Rowing, CrossFit, Bodybuilding, Volleyball, Basketball, American Football, MMA, Wrestling, Rock Climbing

#### EXERCISE 3: LYING DUMBBELL EXTERNAL ROTATION

**Primary Muscles:** Infraspinatus, Teres Minor
**Secondary Muscles:** Posterior Deltoid, Supraspinatus, Subscapularis (stabilizer)
**Type:** Isolation
**Equipment:** Dumbbell, Flat Bench or Mat
**Level:** Beginner

**Scientific Evidence:**
- Sidelying external rotation: Infraspinatus 62% MVIC, Teres minor 67% MVIC
- Critical exercise for rotator cuff strengthening and injury prevention
- High (41-60% MVIC) to very high (>60% MVIC) activation levels achieved
- Proper form essential to prevent deltoid compensation

**References:**
1. Reinold, M. M., et al. (2004). Electromyographic Analysis of the Rotator Cuff and Deltoid Musculature. JOSPT. https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2004.34.7.385
2. Edwards, P. K., et al. (2017). A Systematic Review of Electromyography Studies in Normal Shoulders. JOSPT. https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2017.7271
3. Timing of rotator cuff activation during shoulder external rotation in throwers. PubMed. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16848102/

**Beginner Steps:**
1. Lie on side on flat bench with lower arm supporting head, knees bent for stability
2. Hold light dumbbell in top hand with elbow bent 90 degrees, upper arm against torso
3. Position forearm across abdomen with palm facing down (internal rotation start)
4. Keep elbow fixed against side and rotate forearm upward (external rotation)
5. Raise dumbbell until forearm is perpendicular to floor or slightly beyond
6. Pause 1-2 seconds, then lower with control over 2-3 seconds
7. Complete 12-15 repetitions maintaining strict elbow position

**Advanced Cues:**
1. Keep upper arm pressed firmly against torso - any separation reduces isolation
2. Movement is pure rotation at shoulder joint, not arm lift
3. Use light weight (2-5 lbs) to maintain strict form and prevent compensation
4. Full ROM crucial: start with maximum internal rotation, end with maximum external rotation
5. Eccentric control (3-4 seconds) enhances rotator cuff strengthening

**Sports Applications:**
Baseball, Tennis, Swimming, Volleyball, Javelin, American Football (QB), Cricket, Softball, Basketball, Rock Climbing, CrossFit, All overhead sports

#### EXERCISE 4: LYING DUMBBELL REAR DELT RAISE

**Primary Muscles:** Deltoideus (posterior head)
**Secondary Muscles:** Rhomboideus Major and Minor, Trapezius (middle fibers), Infraspinatus, Teres Minor, Teres Major
**Type:** Isolation
**Equipment:** Dumbbells, Incline Bench
**Level:** Intermediate

**Scientific Evidence:**
- Neutral/hammer grip produces 40.36% eccentric activity vs 36.4% with pronated grip
- Prone positioning superior to standing for isolation due to stabilization
- Chest-supported variation eliminates momentum and lower back stress
- Rear lateral raises rank among top exercises for posterior deltoid isolation

**References:**
1. Different Shoulder Exercises Affect the Activation of Deltoid Portions. PMC. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7706677/
2. Effect of Hand Position on EMG Activity of the Posterior Shoulder Musculature. ResearchGate. https://www.researchgate.net/publication/234098467
3. Activation of the three deltoid muscle portions: A systematic review. ScienceDirect. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1360859222001607

**Beginner Steps:**
1. Lie face down on incline bench set at 30-45 degrees, chest supported, feet on floor
2. Hold dumbbells with neutral grip (palms facing each other), arms hanging beneath shoulders
3. Maintain slight elbow bend (10-20 degrees), engage core for spinal stability
4. Raise dumbbells outward in wide arc, leading with elbows not hands
5. Continue until upper arms parallel to floor or slightly above, perpendicular to torso
6. Squeeze shoulder blades together at top, pause 1-2 seconds
7. Lower slowly with 2-3 second eccentric, complete 12-15 reps

**Advanced Cues:**
1. Chest-supported position eliminates momentum for pure posterior deltoid isolation
2. Neutral/hammer grip produces 10% greater eccentric activation than pronated
3. Focus on horizontal abduction (moving elbows away from midline)
4. Scapular retraction at peak engages rhomboids and middle trapezius
5. Single-arm variation allows identification of bilateral strength imbalances

**Sports Applications:**
Swimming, Baseball, Tennis, Rowing, Volleyball, Wrestling, MMA, Boxing, CrossFit, Bodybuilding, Rock Climbing, American Football

#### EXERCISE 10: CLOSE-GRIP BENCH PRESS

**Primary Muscles:** Triceps Brachii (lateral head, medial head), Pectoralis Major
**Secondary Muscles:** Anterior Deltoid, Triceps Brachii (long head)
**Type:** Compound
**Equipment:** Barbell, Bench, Squat Rack
**Level:** Advanced

**Scientific Evidence:**
- Close-grip bench press: 62% muscle activation (ACE study)
- Primarily targets lateral and medial heads of triceps
- Narrower grip leads to higher triceps activation
- Does not place long head under stretch like dips or overhead variations

**References:**
1. The Effect of Grip Width on Muscle Strength and EMG Activity. PMC. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8296276/
2. Science Says: Best (and Worst) Triceps Exercises. The Barbell. https://thebarbell.com/science-says-the-best-and-worst-triceps-exercises/
3. Weighted Dips vs. Close-Grip Bench Press. BOXROX. https://www.boxrox.com/weighted-dips-vs-close-grip-bench-press/

**Beginner Steps:**
1. Lie on flat bench, position barbell at eye level in rack, feet flat on floor
2. Grip barbell with hands shoulder-width apart or slightly narrower (12-16 inches)
3. Unrack bar with spotter, position over mid-chest
4. Lower bar with control to lower chest/upper abdomen, elbows tucked close to sides
5. Touch chest lightly (don't bounce), press bar back to full elbow extension
6. Maintain tight core and shoulder blade retraction throughout
7. Complete 6-10 repetitions, re-rack with spotter

**Advanced Cues:**
1. Grip width critical: shoulder-width optimizes triceps activation without wrist strain
2. Elbow tuck (~45 degrees from torso) emphasizes triceps vs wide flare
3. Bar path slight arc ending over mid-chest, not directly vertical
4. Long head not maximally engaged due to lack of shoulder flexion
5. Excellent for building pressing strength while emphasizing triceps

**Sports Applications:**
American Football, Rugby, Powerlifting, Bodybuilding, Wrestling, MMA, Boxing, CrossFit, Strongman, Basketball, Volleyball, Track & Field

#### EXERCISE 11: FORWARD LEAN DIPS

**Primary Muscles:** Triceps Brachii (all three heads), Pectoralis Major (sternal head - increased vs upright)
**Secondary Muscles:** Anterior Deltoid, Trapezius, Rhomboids, Core Stabilizers
**Type:** Compound
**Equipment:** Parallel Bars, Dip Station
**Level:** Intermediate

**Scientific Evidence:**
- Weighted dips: High activation in all three triceps heads with emphasis on long head
- Forward lean (10-30 degrees) increases pectoralis major activation significantly
- Dips place long head under greater stretch than close-grip bench press
- Torso angle determines emphasis: forward lean = chest, upright = triceps

**References:**
1. McKenzie, A. L., et al. (2019). Weighted dips EMG study on triceps activation
2. Contreras, B., et al. (2015). Close-grip bench press vs dips for triceps
3. Weighted Dips vs. Close-Grip Bench Press. BOXROX. https://www.boxrox.com/weighted-dips-vs-close-grip-bench-press/

**Beginner Steps:**
1. Grip parallel bars shoulder-width apart, jump or step to support position with arms extended
2. Keep shoulders down and back, engage core
3. **Lean torso forward 20-30 degrees** (key difference from triceps-emphasis dips)
4. Lower body by bending elbows, allowing elbows to flare slightly (45-60 degrees)
5. Descend until shoulders level with elbows or chest feels deep stretch
6. Press through palms to return to starting position, maintain forward lean
7. Complete 5-12 repetitions avoiding swinging

**Advanced Cues:**
1. Forward lean shifts emphasis toward chest while maintaining high triceps activation
2. Long head placed under significant stretch at bottom position
3. Elbows slightly more flared (45-60 degrees) vs triceps dips (close to sides)
4. Controlled eccentric (3 seconds) enhances muscle damage stimulus
5. Add weight belt or dumbbell between feet for progressive overload

**Sports Applications:**
Gymnastics, CrossFit, Bodybuilding, Rock Climbing, American Football, Rugby, Wrestling, MMA, Swimming, Basketball, Volleyball, Powerlifting

#### EXERCISE 14: DIAMOND PUSH-UP

**Primary Muscles:** Triceps Brachii (all three heads - medial, lateral, long)
**Secondary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major, Anterior Deltoid, Core Stabilizers
**Type:** Compound
**Equipment:** Bodyweight, Exercise Mat (optional)
**Level:** Intermediate

**Scientific Evidence:**
- ACE research ranks diamond push-ups as highest triceps-activating bodyweight exercise
- Emphasizes medial and lateral heads with strong overall triceps recruitment
- No significant difference in combined EMG between triangle push-ups, kickbacks, and dips
- One study ranked diamond push-ups as top triceps exercise above close-grip bench press

**References:**
1. ACE-Sponsored EMG Research on Triceps Exercises
2. Electromyographic analysis of the triceps brachii muscle. University of Wisconsin. https://www.krigolsonteaching.com/uploads/4/3/8/4/43848243/sampleemg-triceps.pdf
3. Science Says: Best (and Worst) Triceps Exercises. The Barbell. https://thebarbell.com/science-says-the-best-and-worst-triceps-exercises/

**Beginner Steps:**
1. Start in high plank, place hands close together beneath chest forming diamond with thumbs and index fingers touching
2. Position body in straight line from head to heels, engage core and glutes
3. Keep elbows close to sides (not flared), lower body by bending elbows
4. Descend until chest is 1-2 inches from hands, maintain tight diamond hand position

**Advanced Cues:**

2. All three heads activated but medial and lateral emphasized more than long head

#### EXERCISE 25: SPLIT SQUAT

**Primary Muscles:** Quadriceps (Vastus Lateralis, Vastus Medialis, Vastus Intermedius, Rectus Femoris), Gluteus Maximus
**Secondary Muscles:** Hamstrings (Biceps Femoris, Semitendinosus, Semimembranosus), Gluteus Medius, Adductors, Erector Spinae, Core Stabilizers
**Type:** Compound
**Equipment:** Barbell, Dumbbells, or Bodyweight
**Level:** Advanced

**Scientific Evidence:**
- 2019 study: No significant differences in EMG peak activity of gluteus maximus and vastus lateralis between split squat (SS), single-leg squat (SLS), and rear foot elevated split squat (RFESS)
- Gluteus medius activation: Single-leg squat (81.9% MVIC) > RFESS (54.9% MVIC) > Split Squat (46.2% MVIC)
- Split squats classified as very high activation exercise (>60% MVIC) for gluteus maximus
- Traditional split squats show more quadriceps activation, while torso forward lean increases glute/hamstring (3.8% glute, 6% hamstring increase with forward lean)
- EMG activity higher in hamstrings than quadriceps compared to bilateral squats

**References:**
1. Muscle Activation in Unilateral Barbell Exercises: Implications for Strength Training. PubMed. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29870422/
2. Gluteus Maximus Activation during Common Strength and Hypertrophy Exercises: A Systematic Review. PMC. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7039033/

4. The Activation of Gluteal, Thigh, and Lower Back Muscles in Different Squat Variations. PMC. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7831128/
5. Split Squat Biomechanics and Performance. E3 Rehab. https://e3rehab.com/split-squat-biomechanics/

**Beginner Steps (Bodyweight):**
1. Stand tall with feet hip-width apart, take large step forward with right foot (2-3 feet)
2. Position feet in staggered stance: front foot flat, rear foot on ball of foot (heel elevated)
3. Maintain upright torso, engage core, hands on hips or at chest for balance
4. Lower body straight down by bending both knees simultaneously until front thigh parallel to floor
5. Front knee should track over toes (not past), rear knee descends toward floor (1-2 inches above)
6. Press through front foot heel to drive body back to starting position
7. Complete 10-15 reps on right leg, then switch to left leg

**Beginner Steps (Loaded - Barbell or Dumbbells):**
1. For dumbbells: Hold dumbbells at sides with neutral grip
2. For barbell: Position barbell on upper back (high bar) or rear delts (low bar)
3. Follow same movement pattern as bodyweight variation
4. Start with light weight to master balance and form before progressing load

**Advanced Cues:**
1. Upright torso emphasizes quadriceps; forward torso lean (10-20 degrees) increases glute/hamstring activation by 3-6%
2. Front foot positioning: Heel 2-3 feet in front of rear foot for optimal biomechanics
3. Weight distribution: 70-80% of weight on front leg, 20-30% on rear leg for stability
4. Knee tracking critical: front knee must track over toes, avoid inward collapse (valgus)
5. Split squats show higher hamstring-to-quadriceps EMG ratio vs bilateral squats - excellent for posterior chain development

**Sports Applications:**
American Football, Rugby, Basketball, Soccer, Tennis, Track & Field (sprinting/jumping), Baseball, Volleyball, CrossFit, Bodybuilding, MMA, Wrestling

#### EXERCISE 26: FRONT PLANK (TRIPOD - 1 ARM AND 2 LEGS OR 2 ARMS AND 1 LEG)

**Primary Muscles:** Rectus Abdominis, Transverse Abdominis, External Obliques, Internal Obliques

**Type:** Isometric/Static
**Equipment:** Bodyweight, Exercise Mat (optional)

- Rectus abdominis (RA) and serratus anterior (SA) demonstrated high (41-60% MVIC) EMG activation
- Plank variations can activate core muscles with low compressive forces (1600-1800N), avoiding high lumbar vertebrae compression
- Lifting leg during plank significantly increases EMG activity in glutes, hamstrings, and erector spinae
- 2024-2025 EMG studies rank planks among top choices for muscle activation and functional strength

**References:**
1. Core Muscle Activation With Foam Rolling and Static Planks. PubMed. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35370773/
2. A Comparison of Muscle Activation Among the Front Squat, Overhead Squat, Back Extension and Plank. PMC. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7241624/

**Beginner Steps (Two Arms, One Leg - Easier Variation):**
1. Start in standard front plank position: forearms on floor, elbows under shoulders, body straight line
2. Engage core maximally, ensure neutral spine with no sagging or piking
3. Maintaining plank position, slowly lift one leg off floor while keeping it straight
4. Raise leg until foot is level with hips or slightly above, squeeze glute of lifted leg
5. Hold position for 10-30 seconds maintaining rigid torso and neutral spine
6. Lower leg with control, switch to opposite leg after brief rest
7. Progress by increasing hold duration or adding arm lift

**Advanced Steps (One Arm, Two Legs OR Opposite Arm/Leg - Hardest):**
1. Start in standard front plank on forearms
2. **One Arm Variation:** Slowly lift one forearm off floor, extending arm forward or placing hand on hip
3. **Opposite Arm/Leg (Hardest):** Simultaneously lift opposite arm and leg (e.g., right arm + left leg)
4. Hold position for 10-30 seconds, fighting rotation and maintaining level hips/shoulders
5. Lower with control, switch to opposite side after brief rest

**Advanced Cues:**
1. Tripod variations create massive anti-rotation and anti-extension core demands vs standard plank
2. Gluteus maximus, hamstrings, and erector spinae show very high activation (>61% MVIC) with leg lift
3. External and internal obliques highly activated to prevent torso rotation
4. Opposite arm/leg variation (contralateral) is most challenging due to cross-body stabilization
5. Maintain rigid body position - avoid hip drop, rotation, or spine hyperextension

**Sports Applications:**
American Football, Rugby, MMA, Wrestling, Boxing, Rock Climbing, Gymnastics, CrossFit, Swimming, Rowing, Track & Field, Soccer, Basketball, All Sports (core stability fundamental)
