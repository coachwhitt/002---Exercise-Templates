## BACK CABLE EXERCISES

### 1. Single-Arm Lat Pulldown

**Primary Muscles Worked:**
- Latissimus Dorsi (high unilateral activation)
- Rhomboids Major and Minor (moderate-high activation)
- Middle Trapezius (moderate activation)
- Lower Trapezius (moderate activation)

**Secondary Muscles:**
- Posterior Deltoid
- Biceps Brachii (moderate activation)
- Brachialis
- Brachioradialis
- Core musculature (anti-rotation stabilization - unilateral loading)
- External/Internal Obliques (higher activation than bilateral pulldown)

**Beginner Instructions:**
1. Sit at a lat pulldown machine and adjust the knee pad to secure your lower body in place
2. Attach a single-handle (D-handle) to the cable and grasp it with one hand using a neutral or pronated grip
3. Begin with your working arm fully extended overhead, shoulder slightly elevated, and torso upright
4. Engage your core and maintain a neutral spine throughout the movement
5. Pull the handle down toward your shoulder by driving your elbow down and back, keeping it close to your torso
6. Squeeze your lat and upper back muscles at the bottom of the movement when your hand reaches shoulder height
7. Slowly return to the starting position with control, allowing your arm to fully extend without losing tension
8. Complete all repetitions on one side before switching to the other arm

**Advanced Coaching Cues:**
- Focus on initiating the pull with your lat rather than your biceps by thinking "elbow to hip" instead of "hand to shoulder"
- Maintain scapular depression and retraction at the bottom of the movement to maximize rhomboid and middle trapezius engagement
- Resist trunk rotation during the unilateral pull to maximize core anti-rotation demands and ensure balanced lat development
- Avoid shoulder shrugging at the top of the movement; maintain scapular control throughout the full range of motion
- Experiment with slight torso lean away from the working side (10-15 degrees) to increase lat stretch and activation

   - Research on unilateral training and core stabilization demands, showing increased oblique activation during single-arm pulling exercises

### 2. Standing Cable Row

**Primary Muscles Worked:**
- Latissimus Dorsi (high activation, especially lower fibers)
- Rhomboids Major and Minor (very high activation during scapular retraction)
- Middle Trapezius (very high activation)
- Teres Major

**Secondary Muscles:**
- Posterior Deltoid (moderate activation)
- Biceps Brachii (moderate activation)
- Brachialis
- Brachioradialis
- Erector Spinae (anti-flexion stabilization in standing position)
- Core musculature (rectus abdominis, obliques - isometric anti-extension)
- Hamstrings (isometric hip extensor stabilization)
- Gluteus Maximus (hip stability)

**Beginner Instructions:**
1. Set a cable machine to mid-chest height and attach a straight bar, V-bar, or rope attachment
2. Stand facing the machine with feet hip-to-shoulder width apart, knees slightly bent
3. Grasp the attachment with both hands and step back 2-3 feet to create tension on the cable
4. Begin with arms fully extended, shoulders slightly protracted, and torso upright with a neutral spine
5. Engage your core and maintain a stable, athletic stance throughout the movement
6. Pull the attachment toward your lower chest/upper abdomen by driving your elbows straight back
7. Squeeze your shoulder blades together at the end of the pull, focusing on scapular retraction
8. Slowly return to the starting position with control, allowing your arms to fully extend without losing core tension
9. Avoid leaning back excessively; maintain a relatively upright torso position (slight forward lean is acceptable)

**Advanced Coaching Cues:**
- Initiate the pull with scapular retraction before elbow flexion to maximize rhomboid and middle trapezius engagement
- Maintain constant tension on the cable throughout the entire set; never allow the weight stack to touch down between repetitions
- Keep your chest lifted and shoulders down (depressed) to prevent upper trapezius dominance
- Focus on pulling your elbows as far back as possible rather than simply bending your arms
- Experiment with different attachments (straight bar, rope, V-bar) to target slightly different muscle fiber angles and grip positions

**Scientific References:**

   - EMG study demonstrating seated row produces highest middle trapezius/rhomboid myoelectric activity compared to pulldown variations

3. **Signorile, J. F., Zink, A. J., & Szwed, S. P. (2002).** "A comparative electromyographical investigation of muscle utilization patterns using various hand positions during the lat pull-down." *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research*, 16(4), 539-546.

### 3. Standing Single-Arm Cable Row

**Primary Muscles Worked:**
- Latissimus Dorsi (high unilateral activation, especially lower/lateral fibers)
- Rhomboids Major and Minor (high activation)
- Middle Trapezius (high activation)
- Teres Major

**Secondary Muscles:**
- Posterior Deltoid
- Biceps Brachii (moderate activation)
- Brachialis
- Brachioradialis
- Erector Spinae (anti-flexion and anti-rotation stabilization)
- Core musculature (rectus abdominis, external/internal obliques - very high anti-rotation demand)
- Gluteus Medius (hip stability, anti-lateral flexion)
- Hamstrings (isometric hip stabilization)

**Beginner Instructions:**
1. Set a cable machine to chest height and attach a single-handle (D-handle) to the cable
2. Stand facing the machine with feet hip-to-shoulder width apart in a staggered or parallel stance
3. Grasp the handle with one hand and step back 2-3 feet to create tension on the cable
4. Begin with your working arm fully extended, shoulder slightly protracted, and torso square to the machine
5. Plant your feet firmly and engage your core to prevent trunk rotation during the pull
6. Pull the handle toward your lower ribcage by driving your elbow straight back, keeping it close to your torso
7. Squeeze your shoulder blade back and down at the end of the pull, focusing on scapular retraction
8. Slowly return to the starting position with control, resisting rotation as your arm extends
9. Complete all repetitions on one side before switching to the other arm

**Advanced Coaching Cues:**
- Maintain square shoulders and hips throughout the movement to maximize anti-rotation core engagement
- Create a "long lever" by fully protracting your shoulder blade at the start of each rep for maximum ROM
- Think "elbow to back pocket" to ensure proper lat activation rather than pulling with your biceps
- Experiment with contralateral leg forward (opposite leg from working arm) to increase stability and anti-rotation demands
- Use your non-working arm to brace against your hip or thigh to reinforce core stability and prevent compensatory rotation

## BICEPS FREE WEIGHT EXERCISES - BASIC CURL VARIATIONS

### 4. Alternating Dumbbell Curl

**Primary Muscles Worked:**
- Biceps Brachii - Long Head (emphasis during supinated grip)
- Biceps Brachii - Short Head
- Brachialis (significant activation throughout movement)

**Secondary Muscles:**
- Brachioradialis (moderate activation)
- Anterior Deltoid (stabilization)
- Core musculature (anti-rotation during alternating pattern)
- Forearm flexors (wrist stabilization)

**Beginner Instructions:**
1. Stand with feet hip-to-shoulder width apart holding a dumbbell in each hand at your sides
2. Begin with arms fully extended, palms facing your thighs (neutral grip starting position)
3. Keep your elbows close to your torso throughout the entire movement
4. Curl one dumbbell up while rotating your wrist from neutral to supinated (palm up) as you lift
5. Continue curling until the dumbbell reaches shoulder height and your bicep is fully contracted
6. Squeeze your bicep at the top for a brief moment
7. Lower the dumbbell back to the starting position with control while rotating your wrist back to neutral
8. As one arm lowers, begin curling the opposite arm, creating an alternating rhythm
9. Keep your upper arm stationary; only your forearm should move

**Advanced Coaching Cues:**
- Focus on full supination (palm rotation) at the top of each rep to maximize biceps brachii activation and peak contraction
- Maintain a slight forward lean (5-10 degrees) to keep constant tension on the biceps throughout the ROM
- Control the eccentric (lowering) phase for 2-3 seconds to maximize time under tension and muscle damage stimulus
- Avoid momentum or "swinging" the weights; if you need to rock your torso, the weight is too heavy
- Experiment with pause variations: pause at mid-range (90 degrees) for increased brachialis recruitment

### 5. Alternating Hammer Curl

**Primary Muscles Worked:**
- Brachioradialis (highest activation of all curl variations)
- Brachialis (very high activation with neutral grip)
- Biceps Brachii - Long Head (moderate-high activation)
- Biceps Brachii - Short Head (moderate activation)

**Secondary Muscles:**
- Wrist Extensors (significant stabilization demand)
- Anterior Deltoid (stabilization)
- Core musculature (anti-rotation during alternating pattern)
- Forearm flexors

**Beginner Instructions:**
1. Stand with feet hip-to-shoulder width apart holding a dumbbell in each hand at your sides
2. Begin with arms fully extended and palms facing your torso (neutral/hammer grip)
3. Keep your elbows close to your torso and maintain the neutral grip throughout the movement
4. Curl one dumbbell up toward your shoulder while keeping your palm facing inward
5. Continue curling until the dumbbell reaches shoulder height
6. Squeeze your bicep and forearm muscles at the top for a brief moment
7. Lower the dumbbell back to the starting position with control
8. As one arm lowers, begin curling the opposite arm, creating an alternating rhythm
9. Do not rotate your wrist; maintain the neutral "hammer" grip throughout all repetitions

**Advanced Coaching Cues:**
- Focus on keeping your wrists completely neutral (not flexed or extended) to maximize brachioradialis activation
- Drive your thumb toward your shoulder rather than your pinky to emphasize the proper movement pattern
- Curl slightly across your body (toward opposite pec) to increase brachialis recruitment
- Maintain rigid wrist position throughout; wrist flexion reduces brachioradialis activation
- Use a 3-1-1-3 tempo (3-second eccentric, 1-second pause at bottom, 1-second concentric, 3-second pause at top) to maximize time under tension for forearm development

### 6. Barbell Curl

**Primary Muscles Worked:**
- Biceps Brachii - Long Head (very high activation with supinated grip)
- Biceps Brachii - Short Head (very high activation)
- Brachialis (high activation)

**Secondary Muscles:**
- Brachioradialis (moderate activation with supinated grip)
- Anterior Deltoid (stabilization)
- Forearm Flexors (wrist and grip stabilization)
- Core musculature (anti-extension stabilization)

**Beginner Instructions:**
1. Stand with feet hip-to-shoulder width apart holding a barbell with an underhand (supinated) grip
2. Grip the barbell at shoulder width with palms facing forward
3. Begin with arms fully extended, barbell resting against your upper thighs
4. Keep your elbows close to your torso and maintain a neutral spine throughout
5. Curl the barbell up by flexing your elbows, keeping your upper arms stationary
6. Continue lifting until the barbell reaches shoulder height and your biceps are fully contracted
7. Squeeze your biceps at the top position for a brief moment
8. Lower the barbell back to the starting position with control
9. Avoid swinging or using momentum; keep the movement strict and controlled

**Advanced Coaching Cues:**
- Experiment with grip width: narrow grip (inside shoulder width) emphasizes long head; wide grip emphasizes short head
- Maintain slight elbow flexion at the bottom (don't fully lock out) to keep constant tension on the biceps
- Drive your elbows slightly forward at the top of the movement to increase peak contraction intensity
- Control the eccentric phase for 2-3 seconds to maximize muscle damage and hypertrophy stimulus
- Avoid "breaking" at the wrists (wrist extension) at the top; maintain neutral wrist position throughout

### 8. Bottom/Top/Full Curls (Partial Range Variations)

**Primary Muscles Worked:**
- **Bottom Half Curls:** Biceps Brachii - Long Head (emphasized at stretched position), Brachialis (highest activation 0-90 degrees)
- **Top Half Curls:** Biceps Brachii - Short Head (emphasized at contracted position)
- **Full Range Curls:** Biceps Brachii - Both Heads (balanced activation), Brachialis (comprehensive recruitment)

**Secondary Muscles:**
- Brachioradialis (higher activation in bottom half)
- Anterior Deltoid (stabilization, especially in top half)
- Forearm Flexors (wrist stabilization throughout all variations)
- Core musculature (anti-extension stabilization)

**Beginner Instructions:**
1. **BOTTOM HALF CURLS (0-90 degrees):**
   - Hold dumbbells or barbell with arms fully extended
   - Curl only to 90-degree elbow flexion (forearms parallel to floor)
   - Lower back to full extension with control
   - Focus on the stretched position and initial pull

2. **TOP HALF CURLS (90-135 degrees):**
   - Start with elbows already flexed to 90 degrees
   - Curl from this mid-point to full contraction at shoulder height
   - Lower back to 90 degrees (do not fully extend)
   - Focus on the peak contraction and squeeze

3. **FULL RANGE CURLS (0-135 degrees):**
   - Perform complete curls from full extension to full contraction
   - This serves as the control/comparison exercise
   - Combines benefits of both partial range variations

**Advanced Coaching Cues:**
- **Bottom Half:** Emphasize eccentric control (3-4 seconds) to maximize time under tension at long muscle lengths, which research shows produces superior distal biceps hypertrophy
- **Top Half:** Hold peak contraction for 2-3 seconds and actively squeeze biceps to maximize metabolic stress and short-head activation
- **Progressive Protocol:** Perform bottom-half curls to failure, immediately followed by top-half curls to failure (mechanical drop set technique)
- **Scientific Application:** Bottom-half curls produce +42.8% strength gains vs +19.0% for top-half (research-backed); prioritize bottom-half for strength development
- **Regional Hypertrophy:** Bottom curls emphasize distal biceps growth; top curls emphasize proximal biceps - program both for complete arm development

### 10. Concentration Dumbbell Curl

### 11. Cross Body Hammer Curl

**Primary Muscles Worked:**
- Brachialis (very high activation due to cross-body path)
- Brachioradialis (very high activation with neutral grip)
- Biceps Brachii - Long Head (moderate-high activation, emphasized more than standard hammer curl)
- Biceps Brachii - Short Head (moderate activation)

**Secondary Muscles:**
- Anterior Deltoid (moderate activation during cross-body movement)
- Core musculature (anti-rotation stabilization during cross-body path)
- External/Internal Obliques (higher engagement than standard hammer curl)
- Forearm Flexors and Extensors (wrist stabilization in neutral position)

**Beginner Instructions:**
1. Stand with feet hip-to-shoulder width apart holding a dumbbell in one hand at your side
2. Begin with arm fully extended, palm facing your torso (neutral/hammer grip)
3. Keep your elbow close to your torso at the start of the movement
4. Curl the dumbbell up and across your body toward the opposite shoulder (crossing the midline)

### 15. Incline Alternating Dumbbell Curl

**Primary Muscles Worked:**
- Biceps Brachii - Long Head (very high activation due to shoulder hyperextension/stretched position)
- Biceps Brachii - Short Head (high activation)
- Brachialis (high activation throughout ROM)

**Secondary Muscles:**
- Brachioradialis (moderate activation)
- Anterior Deltoid (stabilization in stretched shoulder position)
- Posterior Deltoid (shoulder stabilization on incline bench)
- Core musculature (stabilization against bench)
- Forearm Flexors (wrist and grip stabilization)

**Beginner Instructions:**
1. Set an incline bench to 45-60 degrees and sit back with your back fully supported against the pad
2. Hold a dumbbell in each hand with arms hanging straight down, fully extended
3. Begin with palms facing forward (supinated grip) or start neutral and supinate as you curl
4. Allow your shoulders to relax back and down, creating a stretch in your biceps at the bottom position
5. Curl one dumbbell up toward your shoulder while keeping your upper arm perpendicular to the floor
6. Continue curling until the dumbbell reaches shoulder height and your bicep is fully contracted
7. Squeeze your bicep at the top, then lower with control back to the fully stretched starting position
8. As one arm lowers, begin curling the opposite arm, creating an alternating pattern
9. Do not allow your elbows to drift forward; keep them pointing toward the floor throughout

**Advanced Coaching Cues:**
- The incline position places your shoulders in hyperextension, which pre-stretches the biceps long head and increases its activation by 15-20% compared to standing curls
- Lower bench angle (45 degrees) = greater long-head stretch; higher angle (60-70 degrees) = less stretch but more strength
- Emphasize full arm extension at bottom with 1-2 second stretch to maximize long-head recruitment
- Control eccentric phase for 3-4 seconds to maximize time under tension at long muscle lengths (research shows superior hypertrophy)
- Allow full supination at top of movement; actively rotate pinky outward to maximize peak contraction

### 17. Incline Dumbbell Curl

**Primary Muscles Worked:**
- Biceps Brachii - Long Head (very high activation - maximum of all curl variations due to stretched position)
- Biceps Brachii - Short Head (high activation)
- Brachialis (high activation throughout ROM)

**Secondary Muscles:**
- Brachioradialis (moderate activation)
- Anterior Deltoid (shoulder stabilization in hyperextended position)
- Posterior Deltoid (shoulder stabilization against bench)
- Core musculature (stabilization against bench)
- Forearm Flexors (wrist and grip stabilization)

**Beginner Instructions:**
1. Set an incline bench to 45-60 degrees and sit back with your back fully supported against the pad
2. Hold a dumbbell in each hand with arms hanging straight down, fully extended
3. Begin with palms facing forward (supinated grip) throughout the movement
4. Allow your shoulders to relax back and down, creating a stretch in your biceps at the bottom position
5. Curl both dumbbells up simultaneously toward your shoulders
6. Keep your upper arms perpendicular to the floor; do not allow your elbows to drift forward
7. Continue curling until the dumbbells reach shoulder height and your biceps are fully contracted
8. Squeeze your biceps at the top, then lower both dumbbells with control back to the fully stretched starting position
9. Maintain constant tension; do not rest at the bottom between repetitions

**Advanced Coaching Cues:**

- Bench angle critical: 45° = maximum stretch and long-head emphasis; 60° = moderate stretch with better strength curve; 70° = minimal advantage over standing curls
- Emphasize 1-2 second stretch at bottom position to maximize time under tension at long muscle lengths (research shows superior hypertrophy)
- Full supination throughout movement; actively rotate pinkies outward at top for maximum peak contraction
- Avoid allowing elbows to drift forward during curl; keep upper arms perpendicular to floor to maintain constant biceps tension

- External/Internal Obliques (anti-rotation and stability)
- Hip Flexors (kneeling position stabilization)
- Gluteus Maximus (hip stabilization in tall kneeling position)

**Beginner Instructions:**
1. Set a cable machine to the lowest position and attach a straight bar or EZ-bar attachment

3. Position yourself 2-3 feet from the machine to create tension on the cable
4. Grasp the bar with an underhand (supinated) grip at shoulder width
5. Begin with arms fully extended, bar at upper thigh level, with your torso completely upright

- The kneeling position is inherently unstable compared to standing, increasing stabilizer muscle recruitment throughout movement
- Use lighter loads (15-20% less) than standing cable curls due to increased stability demands and strict form requirements

### 20. Lying Cable Curl

**Beginner Instructions:**

- Position elbows to create slight shoulder hyperextension (elbows angled back 15-30 degrees from vertical) to maximize long-head stretch
- Control eccentric phase for 3-4 seconds to capitalize on stretched-position time under tension for superior hypertrophy
