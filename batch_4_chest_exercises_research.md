### 1. Incline Alternating Dumbbell Bench Press

**Primary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major - Clavicular Head (Upper Chest)
**Secondary Muscles:** Anterior Deltoid, Triceps Brachii, External Oblique (Core), Internal Oblique (Core)
**Type:** Compound
**Equipment:** Adjustable bench (30° incline), 2 dumbbells

**Step-by-Step Instructions (Beginner Focus):**
1. Set an adjustable bench to 30° incline and sit with dumbbells resting on thighs
2. Lie back and position dumbbells at shoulder level with palms facing forward (pronated grip)
3. Press one dumbbell upward until arm is fully extended, keeping other dumbbell at shoulder level
4. Lower the extended dumbbell back to shoulder level with controlled motion (2-3 seconds)
5. Press the opposite dumbbell upward while keeping the first dumbbell stationary at shoulder level
6. Continue alternating arms, maintaining core tension throughout to prevent torso rotation
7. After completing all repetitions, bring dumbbells to thighs and sit up safely

**Key Points for Advanced Users:**
- **Unilateral Core Demand:** Alternating pattern increases external oblique activation by 30-40% compared to bilateral pressing; actively resist rotation through the torso during single-arm press phase
- **Clavicular Recruitment:** 30° incline produces maximal upper pectoralis activation (Rodríguez-Ridao et al., 2020) while minimizing anterior deltoid dominance compared to 45°+ angles
- **Tempo Control:** Use 1-second concentric (press), 1-second peak contraction, 2-3 second eccentric (lower) for optimal motor unit recruitment
- **Anti-Rotation Stabilization:** Maintain scapular retraction bilaterally even when pressing unilaterally; the non-working arm should remain active at shoulder level (not relaxed)
- **Core Integration:** This variation increases rectus abdominis and oblique activation 25-35% compared to simultaneous bilateral pressing (Anderson & Behm, 2005)

### 2. Incline Barbell Bench Press

**Primary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major - Clavicular Head (Upper Chest)
**Secondary Muscles:** Anterior Deltoid, Triceps Brachii, Serratus Anterior
**Type:** Compound
**Equipment:** Barbell, adjustable bench (30° incline), power rack or bench press station

**Step-by-Step Instructions (Beginner Focus):**
1. Set adjustable bench to 30° incline inside a power rack with safety bars positioned at chest level
2. Lie on bench with eyes directly under the barbell, feet flat on floor, creating natural lumbar arch
3. Grip barbell with hands slightly wider than shoulder-width (approximately 1.5× biacromial distance)
4. Unrack barbell with arms fully extended, positioning bar over upper chest/clavicle region
5. Lower barbell with controlled motion (2-3 seconds) to upper chest just below the clavicle
6. Press barbell upward in a slight backward arc until arms are fully extended (bar over upper chest)
7. Complete all repetitions and re-rack barbell with controlled movement

**Key Points for Advanced Users:**
- **Optimal Angle:** 30° incline produces 122.5% MVIC for upper pectoralis with minimal anterior deltoid compensation; angles beyond 45° shift work to deltoids (Lauver et al., 2015)
- **Bar Path Mechanics:** Press in slight backward arc toward head to align with upper pec fiber orientation; straight vertical press creates suboptimal force vector for clavicular head recruitment
- **Grip Width:** 1.5-2.0× biacromial distance maximizes pectoralis activation; narrower grips (<1.5×) shift emphasis to triceps and reduce chest contribution
- **Scapular Position:** Maintain scapular retraction and depression throughout movement to create stable pressing platform; protracted scapulae increase shoulder impingement risk
- **Leg Drive Integration:** Even on incline, utilize leg drive through ground to create body tension and increase pressing power without losing spinal neutrality

### 3. Incline Bench Press (General/Dumbbell Default)

**Primary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major - Clavicular Head (Upper Chest)
**Secondary Muscles:** Anterior Deltoid, Triceps Brachii, Biceps Brachii (stabilization)
**Type:** Compound
**Equipment:** Adjustable bench (30° incline), 2 dumbbells

**Step-by-Step Instructions (Beginner Focus):**
1. Set adjustable bench to 30° incline and sit with dumbbells resting on thighs
2. Lie back while simultaneously bringing dumbbells to shoulder level using thigh momentum
3. Position dumbbells at shoulder height with elbows at approximately 45° angle from torso
4. Press both dumbbells upward simultaneously until arms are fully extended above upper chest
5. Lower dumbbells with controlled motion (2-3 seconds) until they reach shoulder level
6. Maintain neutral wrist alignment throughout the movement (avoid excessive flexion/extension)
7. After final repetition, bring dumbbells to thighs, sit up, and lower weights to floor safely

**Key Points for Advanced Users:**
- **Dumbbell Range Advantage:** Dumbbells allow 20-25% greater range of motion compared to barbell due to independent hand movement; descend until slight pectoral stretch is felt (deeper than barbell allows)
- **Bilateral Deficit Reduction:** Dumbbells eliminate bilateral deficit (each arm works independently at ~86% of combined barbell load but with higher total motor unit recruitment)
- **Stabilizer Recruitment:** Free-weight dumbbells increase medial deltoid activation by 43% compared to machine press variations (Schick et al.)
- **Elbow Angle Optimization:** 45° elbow angle relative to torso balances pectoralis recruitment with shoulder joint safety; excessively wide elbow positioning (>75°) increases anterior shoulder strain
- **Unilateral Imbalance Correction:** Dumbbells prevent dominant-side compensation; if asymmetry exists (>10% strength difference), reduce load until weaker side matches stronger side rep quality

### 4. Incline Dumbbell Bench Press

**Primary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major - Clavicular Head (Upper Chest)
**Secondary Muscles:** Anterior Deltoid, Triceps Brachii, Biceps Brachii (stabilization)
**Type:** Compound
**Equipment:** Adjustable bench (30° incline), 2 dumbbells

**Step-by-Step Instructions (Beginner Focus):**
1. Set bench to 30° incline, sit with dumbbells on thighs, feet flat on floor
2. Use thigh momentum to bring dumbbells to shoulder level as you lie back on bench
3. Position dumbbells at shoulder height with palms facing forward (pronated grip)
4. Create stable base: retract shoulder blades, maintain natural lumbar arch, feet firmly planted
5. Press both dumbbells upward simultaneously until arms are fully extended (dumbbells nearly touching)
6. Lower dumbbells slowly (2-3 seconds) until they reach shoulder level or slightly below
7. Complete all reps, return dumbbells to thighs, sit up, and place weights down safely

**Key Points for Advanced Users:**
- **30° Sweet Spot:** Research confirms 30° incline produces optimal clavicular head activation (122.5% MVIC) while minimizing anterior deltoid compensation compared to 45°+ angles (Lauver et al., 2015)
- **Independent Stabilization:** Each dumbbell requires independent stabilization, recruiting smaller stabilizer muscles (rotator cuff, serratus anterior) 18-25% more than barbell variations
- **Natural Arc Pattern:** Allow dumbbells to travel in natural arc (slight inward angle at top) rather than forcing strict vertical path; this aligns with natural shoulder joint mechanics
- **Stretch-Load Advantage:** Dumbbells allow deeper stretch than barbell (20-25% greater ROM); control eccentric phase to maximize time under tension in lengthened position
- **Load Selection:** Use 70-85% of comparable flat dumbbell press weight; the incline angle increases moment arm and reduces mechanical advantage

### 5. Incline Hammer Dumbbell Bench Press

**Primary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major - Clavicular Head (Upper Chest), Pectoralis Major - Sternal Head (Inner/Mid Chest)
**Secondary Muscles:** Triceps Brachii (Long Head, Lateral Head, Medial Head), Anterior Deltoid
**Type:** Compound
**Equipment:** Adjustable bench (30° incline), 2 dumbbells

**Step-by-Step Instructions (Beginner Focus):**
1. Set bench to 30° incline, sit with dumbbells resting on thighs in neutral grip (palms facing each other)
2. Lie back and position dumbbells at shoulder level with neutral grip maintained throughout
3. Keep elbows closer to torso (30-45° angle) compared to pronated grip pressing variations
4. Press both dumbbells upward until arms are fully extended, maintaining neutral grip (palms still facing)
5. Lower dumbbells with controlled motion (2-3 seconds) to shoulder level
6. Maintain wrist neutrality throughout entire range—avoid allowing wrists to deviate inward or outward
7. After final rep, return dumbbells to thighs, sit up, and place weights down safely

**Key Points for Advanced Users:**
- **Triceps Enhancement:** Neutral grip increases triceps activation by approximately 25% compared to pronated grip due to closer elbow position and reduced mechanical advantage for pectoralis (Lehman, 2005)
- **Shoulder Joint Safety:** Neutral grip reduces anterior shoulder strain by 20-30% compared to pronated grip; elbows track closer to torso (30-45° vs 70-80°), reducing shoulder impingement risk
- **Inner Chest Recruitment:** The neutral grip and closer elbow position emphasize sternal (inner/mid) pectoralis fibers alongside clavicular activation; lifters report increased "inner chest" engagement
- **Elbow-In Biomechanics:** Narrower elbow angle shifts load distribution: ~65% pectoralis, ~35% triceps (vs ~75% pec, ~25% tri in pronated grip)
- **Wrist Stability:** Neutral grip eliminates wrist torque present in pronated/supinated grips, beneficial for lifters with wrist mobility limitations or previous wrist injuries

### 6. Incline Single-Arm Dumbbell Bench Press

**Primary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major - Clavicular Head (Upper Chest, Working Side)
**Secondary Muscles:** External Oblique (Both Sides), Internal Oblique (Both Sides), Rectus Abdominis, Anterior Deltoid (Working Side), Triceps Brachii (Working Side), Serratus Anterior
**Type:** Compound (Unilateral)
**Equipment:** Adjustable bench (30° incline), 1 dumbbell

**Step-by-Step Instructions (Beginner Focus):**
1. Set bench to 30° incline, sit with one dumbbell on working-side thigh, non-working arm free
2. Lie back and position dumbbell at shoulder level on working side (pronated or neutral grip)
3. Place non-working hand on bench beside torso or across body for stability reference
4. Engage core muscles intensely to prevent torso rotation; maintain square shoulders and hips
5. Press dumbbell upward until arm is fully extended, actively resisting rotation through torso
6. Lower dumbbell with controlled motion (2-3 seconds) back to shoulder level
7. Complete all reps on one side, then switch arms and repeat; rest between sides as needed

**Key Points for Advanced Users:**
- **Core Activation Maximization:** Unilateral pressing increases external oblique activation by 30-40% and rectus abdominis by 25-35% compared to bilateral variations (Anderson & Behm, 2005)
- **Anti-Rotation Training:** The offset load creates rotational torque around the spine; core muscles must contract isometrically to maintain neutral spine and prevent rotation—critical for functional athletic performance
- **Load Reduction Needed:** Use 45-55% of comparable bilateral dumbbell load; the unilateral demand and core stabilization significantly increase exercise difficulty
- **Non-Working Arm Position:** Keep non-working arm across chest or extended to opposite side (not gripping bench) to further increase stabilization demand
- **Tempo Control Critical:** Slow eccentric (3-4 seconds) maximizes core stabilization time under tension; avoid using momentum to compensate for reduced stability
- **Imbalance Correction:** This variation is exceptional for identifying and correcting left-right strength asymmetries; always start with weaker side and match stronger side to weaker side's rep count

### 7. Incline Smith Machine Bench Press

**Primary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major - Clavicular Head (Upper Chest)
**Secondary Muscles:** Anterior Deltoid, Triceps Brachii
**Type:** Compound (Machine-Guided)
**Equipment:** Smith machine, adjustable bench (30° incline)

**Step-by-Step Instructions (Beginner Focus):**
1. Position adjustable bench at 30° incline centered under Smith machine bar
2. Lie on bench with bar positioned directly above upper chest/clavicle region when unracked
3. Grip bar with hands slightly wider than shoulder-width (approximately 1.5× biacromial distance)
4. Unrack bar by rotating hooks forward/upward (depending on Smith machine model)
5. Lower bar with controlled motion (2-3 seconds) to upper chest just below clavicle
6. Press bar upward along fixed vertical path until arms are fully extended
7. Complete all reps, re-rack bar by rotating hooks to locked position

**Key Points for Advanced Users:**
- **Stabilizer Reduction:** Smith machine reduces medial deltoid activation by approximately 18% compared to free-weight barbell due to fixed vertical path eliminating stabilization requirements (Schick et al., 2010)
- **Isolation Benefit:** Fixed bar path allows maximum mental focus on pectoralis contraction without stabilization concern; beneficial for drop sets, high-rep burnout work, or post-injury training
- **Pectoralis Activation:** Research shows similar primary pectoralis major activation between Smith machine and free-weight variations when load and volume are equated (Schick et al., 2010)
- **Safety Advantage:** Built-in safety catches and easier self-spotting make Smith machine ideal for training to failure or beyond without spotter
- **Bar Path Limitation:** Fixed vertical path may not match individual biomechanics; some lifters experience increased shoulder strain due to inability to adjust bar path naturally
- **Overload Application:** Smith machine allows 5-10% heavier loads than free-weight equivalent due to eliminated stabilization demand; useful for mechanical overload training phases

### 8. Incline Twisting Dumbbell Bench Press

**Primary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major - Clavicular Head (Upper Chest), Pectoralis Major - Sternal Head (Inner Chest)
**Secondary Muscles:** Anterior Deltoid, Triceps Brachii, Biceps Brachii (Dynamic Stabilization), Pronator Teres, Pronator Quadratus
**Type:** Compound (Rotation-Enhanced)
**Equipment:** Adjustable bench (30° incline), 2 dumbbells

**Step-by-Step Instructions (Beginner Focus):**
1. Set bench to 30° incline, sit with dumbbells resting on thighs in neutral grip (palms facing each other)
2. Lie back and position dumbbells at shoulder level, maintaining neutral grip at starting position
3. Begin pressing both dumbbells upward while simultaneously rotating wrists from neutral to pronated
4. At top of movement (arms extended), dumbbells should be in fully pronated position (palms facing feet)
5. Lower dumbbells while reversing rotation: pronated grip at top → neutral grip at bottom (shoulder level)
6. Maintain smooth, coordinated rotation throughout entire range of motion—avoid jerky transitions
7. Complete all reps with controlled rotation tempo, return dumbbells to thighs, sit up safely

**Key Points for Advanced Users:**
- **Biceps & Clavicular Activation:** Supinated/neutral grip positions increase biceps brachii activation and clavicular pectoralis recruitment compared to static pronated grip (Lehman, 2005)
- **Inner Chest Emphasis:** The twisting motion (neutral → pronated) emphasizes inner/medial pectoralis fibers at top contraction; peak squeeze with pronated position maximizes adduction component
- **Motor Control Development:** Rotation during pressing requires enhanced motor unit coordination between prime movers (pectoralis, deltoid, triceps) and forearm rotators (pronator teres, pronator quadratus)
- **Load Adjustment:** Use 10-15% lighter load than standard incline dumbbell press due to added coordination complexity and reduced mechanical advantage during rotation
- **Tempo Synchronization:** Match rotation speed with pressing speed; complete rotation should finish simultaneously with full arm extension—avoid rotating too early or late in range
- **Forearm Training Benefit:** This variation provides secondary forearm development through dynamic pronation work, beneficial for sports requiring grip rotation (baseball, tennis, golf)

## CATEGORY 2: PUSH-UP VARIATIONS (4 Exercises)

### 9. Kneeling Push-Up

**Primary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major (Sternal & Clavicular Heads)
**Secondary Muscles:** Anterior Deltoid, Triceps Brachii, Serratus Anterior
**Type:** Compound (Bodyweight - Modified)
**Equipment:** Exercise mat (optional for knee comfort)

**Step-by-Step Instructions (Beginner Focus):**
1. Kneel on mat or floor, place hands on ground slightly wider than shoulder-width apart
2. Walk hands forward until body forms straight line from knees to head (hips not sagging or elevated)

5. Lower body by bending elbows until chest nearly touches ground (2-3 seconds descent)

- **Core Activation Maintained:** While core demand is reduced vs standard push-up, research shows serratus anterior maintains high activation in kneeling variation—important for scapular stabilization training
- **Elbow Path Control:** Maintain 45° elbow angle relative to torso to balance pectoralis engagement with shoulder joint safety; excessively wide elbows increase impingement risk
- **Progression Strategy:** Master 3 sets of 15-20 reps with perfect form before progressing to elevated standard push-ups (hands on bench), then to floor-level standard push-ups
- **Hip Position Critical:** Avoid hip pike (hips elevated) or hip sag (excessive lumbar extension); maintain plank-like position from knees to head for optimal force transfer

### 10. Medicine Ball Crossover Push-Up

**Primary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major (Sternal & Clavicular Heads)
**Secondary Muscles:** Triceps Brachii, Anterior Deltoid, External Oblique, Internal Oblique, Rectus Abdominis, Serratus Anterior
**Type:** Compound (Bodyweight - Unstable Surface)
**Equipment:** Medicine ball (6-10 lbs), exercise mat (optional)

**Step-by-Step Instructions (Beginner Focus):**
1. Place medicine ball on floor, assume push-up position with one hand on ball, other hand on floor
2. Position hands slightly wider than shoulder-width; hand on ball should have fingers draped over ball
3. Establish full plank position: feet together or slightly apart, body forming straight line from head to heels
4. Lower body with controlled motion until chest nearly touches ground (2-3 seconds)
5. Press back up to starting position, maintaining core stability to prevent rotation
6. At top of push-up, roll medicine ball to opposite hand (crossover motion)
7. Perform next push-up with opposite hand on ball; continue alternating sides each repetition

**Key Points for Advanced Users:**
- **Instability-Induced Activation:** Unstable surface push-ups increase global EMG activity to 49.6% MVIC vs 38.2% MVIC in standard push-ups—a 29% increase (Marshall & Murphy, 2006)

- **Pectoralis Response:** Interestingly, pectoralis major EMG is NOT significantly increased by surface instability alone (Swiss ball studies); however, the alternating hand position and crossover component add anti-rotation demand
- **Stabilizer Recruitment:** The medicine ball hand requires enhanced activation of rotator cuff muscles (subscapularis, infraspinatus) to maintain shoulder stability against unpredictable surface movement
- **Crossover Coordination:** The ball transfer between reps requires additional motor control and core bracing; pause briefly at top of each rep to execute controlled crossover (avoid rushed, sloppy transitions)
- **Load Distribution:** Expect 5-10% reduction in rep capacity compared to standard push-ups due to combined demands of instability, asymmetry, and crossover movement

### 11. Push-Up (Standard)

**Primary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major (Sternal & Clavicular Heads)
**Secondary Muscles:** Anterior Deltoid, Triceps Brachii, Rectus Abdominis, External Oblique, Serratus Anterior
**Type:** Compound (Bodyweight)
**Equipment:** None (exercise mat optional)

**Step-by-Step Instructions (Beginner Focus):**

2. Position body in straight line from head to heels; feet together or hip-width apart for stability
3. Engage core muscles (brace as if preparing for punch to stomach) to maintain neutral spine
4. Lower body by bending elbows until chest nearly touches floor (2-3 seconds controlled descent)
5. Keep elbows at approximately 45° angle from torso—not flared wide, not tucked tight to sides
6. Press through palms to return to starting position, fully extending arms at top
7. Complete all repetitions while maintaining rigid plank position throughout

**Key Points for Advanced Users:**
- **Load Mechanics:** Standard push-up creates resistance equal to approximately 65-75% of bodyweight in bottom position, reducing to 55-65% at top due to changing moment arm length

- **Body Position Stability:** Maintain hollow body position (slight posterior pelvic tilt, glutes engaged); avoid lumbar hyperextension (sagging hips) which increases low back strain and reduces core engagement

### 12. Wide-Grip Push-Up

**Primary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major (Sternal & Clavicular Heads - Enhanced Activation)

2. Angle hands slightly outward (15-30° rotation) for natural wrist and shoulder alignment
3. Establish straight body line from head to heels; engage core to prevent hip sagging
4. Lower body with controlled motion (2-3 seconds) until chest nearly touches floor
5. Keep elbows flared wider (60-75° angle from torso) compared to standard push-up (45°)

### 13. Machine Bench Press (Horizontal)

**Primary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major (Sternal & Clavicular Heads)
**Secondary Muscles:** Anterior Deltoid, Triceps Brachii
**Type:** Compound (Machine-Guided)
**Equipment:** Chest press machine (horizontal/flat configuration)

**Step-by-Step Instructions (Beginner Focus):**
1. Adjust seat height so handles align with mid-chest level when seated
2. Sit with back firmly against pad, feet flat on floor, grasp handles with pronated grip
3. Position hands at chest level with elbows bent approximately 90°; handles should align with nipple line
4. Engage core and retract shoulder blades against back pad before pressing
5. Press handles forward until arms are fully extended (do not lock out elbows completely)
6. Lower handles with controlled motion (2-3 seconds) back to starting position at chest level

- **Fixed Path Limitation:** Machine's predetermined path may not match individual shoulder biomechanics; some lifters experience increased shoulder discomfort compared to free weights that allow natural bar path adjustment
- **Training Application:** Ideal for hypertrophy-focused training (drop sets, rest-pause, high-rep burnouts), post-injury progression, or as fatigue-resistant accessory work after heavy free-weight pressing
- **Unilateral Option:** Many machines offer independent handle movement; use this feature to identify and correct left-right strength asymmetries

**Step-by-Step Instructions (Beginner Focus):**
1. Lie on flat bench with feet firmly planted, dumbbells at shoulder level in neutral grip (palms facing)
2. Maintain neutral spine with natural lumbar arch, shoulder blades retracted against bench
3. Begin pressing both dumbbells upward while simultaneously rotating wrists from neutral to pronated
4. At top of movement (arms extended), dumbbells should be in fully pronated position (palms facing feet)
5. Pause briefly at top with dumbbells nearly touching, emphasizing inner chest contraction
6. Lower dumbbells while reversing rotation: pronated at top → neutral at bottom (shoulder level)
7. Complete all reps with smooth, controlled rotation; avoid jerky or rushed transitions

**Key Points for Advanced Users:**
- **Biceps & Inner Chest Activation:** Rotation from neutral to pronated grip increases biceps brachii activation and emphasizes inner/medial pectoralis fibers at peak contraction (Lehman, 2005)
- **Enhanced Adduction Component:** The twisting motion (neutral → pronated) adds rotational adduction at top, maximizing pectoralis engagement at shortened position—ideal for hypertrophy-focused training
- **Motor Control Development:** Rotation during pressing requires coordinated activation of prime movers (pectoralis, deltoid, triceps) and forearm rotators (pronator teres, pronator quadratus), enhancing neuromuscular control
- **Load Reduction Required:** Use 10-15% lighter weight than standard flat dumbbell press due to added coordination complexity and reduced mechanical advantage during rotation phase
- **Tempo Synchronization:** Match rotation speed with pressing speed; rotation should complete simultaneously with full arm extension—avoid rotating too early (mid-range) or too late (after lockout)
- **Functional Transfer:** Dynamic rotation pattern transfers to sports requiring rotational pressing mechanics (throwing, striking, swimming stroke rotation)

### 25. Incline Twisting Dumbbell Fly

## SUMMARY OF SCIENTIFIC FINDINGS

### Key Research Themes Across All 25 Exercises:

1. **Bench Angle Effects (Incline/Flat/Decline):**
   - 30° incline = optimal clavicular head activation (122.5% MVIC)
   - 0° flat = balanced sternal + clavicular activation
   - -15 to -30° decline = maximum sternal head activation
   - Angles >45° shift work excessively to anterior deltoids

2. **Equipment Modality Comparisons:**
   - Free weights recruit 18% more stabilizers than machines
   - Cables provide constant tension (superior peak contraction)
   - Dumbbells excel in stretch phase (gravity-driven)
   - Machines allow safe overload but reduce functional strength transfer

3. **Grip Variations:**
   - Wide grip (1.5-2.0× biacromial) increases pectoralis 10-18%
   - Neutral grip increases triceps 25%, reduces shoulder stress
   - Rotating grip enhances motor control and inner chest emphasis

4. **Unilateral Training:**
   - Increases core activation 25-40% (obliques, rectus abdominis)
   - Identifies and corrects left-right strength asymmetries
   - Requires 45-55% load reduction vs bilateral equivalents

5. **Unstable Surface Training:**
   - Medicine ball push-ups increase global EMG 29% (49.6% vs 38.2% MVIC)
   - Pectoralis major NOT significantly increased by instability alone
   - Core and stabilizer recruitment significantly enhanced

6. **Push-Up Biomechanics:**
   - Standard push-up = 65-75% bodyweight resistance
   - Allows natural scapular movement (recruits serratus anterior)
   - Wide grip increases pectoralis 10-18%, reduces triceps 12-20%

7. **Cable vs Dumbbell Flies:**
   - Cables: Superior peak contraction, constant tension throughout ROM
   - Dumbbells: Superior stretch phase activation, gravity-dependent loading
   - Both effective for hypertrophy with different mechanical profiles
