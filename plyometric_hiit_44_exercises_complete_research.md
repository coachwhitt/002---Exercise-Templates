### EXERCISE 7: BOX JUMPS

**Primary Muscles:** Quadriceps Femoris (Rectus Femoris, Vastus Lateralis, Vastus Medialis, Vastus Intermedius); Gluteus Maximus; Gluteus Medius

**Secondary Muscles:** Hamstrings (Biceps Femoris, Semitendinosus, Semimembranosus); Gastrocnemius; Soleus; Erector Spinae; Core (Rectus Abdominis, Obliques)

**Type:** Compound

**Equipment:** Plyometric Box (12-36 inches)

**Level:** Intermediate

**Body Region:** Lower

**Movement:** Explosive

**Modality:** Plyometric

**Functional Groups:** Quads_Hamstrings_Glutes; Explosive_Power; Full_Lower_Body

**Steps for Beginners:**
1. Stand 6-12 inches away from box with feet hip-width apart. 2. Lower into quarter squat position (30-45° knee flexion) with arms back. 3. Explosively swing arms forward and up while extending hips, knees, and ankles simultaneously. 4. Drive through entire foot to propel body upward onto box. 5. Land softly on box with both feet simultaneously, knees slightly bent (20-30° flexion). 6. Stand fully upright on box, achieving full hip and knee extension. 7. Step down carefully one foot at a time, reset position, and repeat.

**Advanced Key Points:**
1. Triple Extension Focus: Achieve complete ankle, knee, and hip extension during takeoff for maximum power output and height. 2. Arm Drive Coordination: Aggressive arm swing from low to high position adds 10-15% jump height through momentum transfer. 3. Landing Mechanics: Land with soft knees maintaining hip-knee-ankle alignment; avoid knee valgus collapse or excessive forward lean. 4. Box Height Progression: Start with 12-18 inch box; progress to 24-36 inches only after mastering technique and landing control. 5. Step-Down vs Jump-Down: Always step down to preserve eccentric loading capacity for subsequent reps; jump-downs increase injury risk and fatigue.

**Scientific Reference:**
Muyor et al. (2020). Electromyographic activity in the gluteus medius, gluteus maximus, biceps femoris, vastus lateralis, vastus medialis and rectus femoris during exercises. PLOS ONE; Ebben WP et al. (2008). Evaluation of plyometric intensity using electromyography. J Strength Cond Res; PMC 7727409. Gluteal recruitment during jumping tasks shows significant activation; Normative muscle activation patterns during countermovement jumps (2025 study)

**Sports Tags:** Basketball, Volleyball, CrossFit, American Football, Track & Field, Soccer, Rugby, Ice Hockey, Alpine Skiing, Tennis, Parkour, General Fitness

**Training_Goal:** Power

**Movement_Pattern:** Jump_Vertical

**Equipment_Type:** Plyometric Box

### EXERCISE 8: BROAD JUMPS (STANDING LONG JUMP)

**Primary Muscles:** Quadriceps Femoris (Rectus Femoris, Vastus Lateralis, Vastus Medialis, Vastus Intermedius); Gluteus Maximus; Hip Extensors

**Secondary Muscles:** Hamstrings (Biceps Femoris, Semitendinosus, Semimembranosus); Gastrocnemius; Soleus; Tibialis Anterior; Erector Spinae; Rectus Abdominis

**Type:** Compound

**Equipment:** Bodyweight

**Level:** Intermediate

**Body Region:** Lower

**Movement:** Explosive

**Modality:** Plyometric

**Functional Groups:** Quads_Hamstrings_Glutes; Explosive_Power; Full_Lower_Body

**Steps for Beginners:**
1. Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, toes slightly outward. 2. Swing arms back while lowering into quarter squat (knees at 90-120° angle). 3. Explosively extend hips, knees, and ankles while swinging arms forward and up. 4. Drive horizontally forward, focusing on distance rather than height. 5. Extend body fully in flight with arms reaching forward. 6. Land on both feet simultaneously with knees bent (30-45° flexion) to absorb impact. 7. Measure distance from starting line to back of heels upon landing.

**Advanced Key Points:**
1. Hip and Ankle Contribution: Research shows hip and ankle muscles contribute 45.9% and 50.2% respectively to propulsion; knee contributes only 3.9%. 2. Arm Swing Mechanics: Powerful arm drive forward increases jump distance 10-15%; coordinate with leg extension for maximum momentum transfer. 3. Takeoff Angle Optimization: Aim for 30-45° takeoff angle for maximum horizontal distance; too vertical reduces distance. 4. Landing Control: Absorb landing through hip hinge and knee flexion; avoid excessive forward lean or sitting back. 5. Foot Placement Strategy: Straddle foot placement (slight external rotation) improves performance by 13.58 cm (5.18%) vs parallel stance.

**Scientific Reference:**
Wakai M & Linthorne NP (2013). Biomechanical analysis of standing long jump from varying starting positions. J Strength Cond Res 27(10):2674-2684; PMC 10622972. Effects of muscle strength in different parts on standing long jump distance using surface EMG; PMC 9010215. Biomechanical research on long jump take-off muscle activation; MDPI (2025). Regional muscle strength and mass effects on standing long jump performance

**Training_Goal:** Power

**Movement_Pattern:** Jump_Horizontal

### EXERCISE 9: DEPTH JUMPS

**Primary Muscles:** Quadriceps Femoris (all four heads); Gluteus Maximus; Gastrocnemius

**Secondary Muscles:** Hamstrings (Biceps Femoris, Semitendinosus, Semimembranosus); Soleus; Hip Adductors; Erector Spinae; Core Stabilizers

**Type:** Compound

**Equipment:** Plyometric Box (12-24 inches)

**Level:** Advanced

**Body Region:** Lower

**Movement:** Explosive

**Modality:** Plyometric

**Functional Groups:** Quads_Hamstrings_Glutes; Explosive_Power; Eccentric_Loading

**Steps for Beginners:**
1. Stand on edge of box (12-18 inches height for beginners) with toes at edge. 2. Step off box (do not jump) allowing gravity to pull body downward. 3. Land on both feet simultaneously with slight knee flexion (20-30°). 4. Upon ground contact, immediately explode upward into maximal vertical jump. 5. Minimize ground contact time to less than 0.2 seconds (amortization phase). 6. Land from jump with controlled knee flexion and reset. 7. Step back onto box and repeat for prescribed repetitions (typically 3-5 per set).

**Advanced Key Points:**
1. Eccentric Loading Emphasis: Depth jumps create highest eccentric forces in muscle hierarchy; faster eccentric = greater concentric force production. 2. Amortization Phase Critical: Minimize ground contact time (<0.2s) to maximize stretch-shortening cycle benefits; longer contact reduces plyometric effect. 3. Box Height Progression: Start 12-18 inches; progress to 24-36 inches only when ground contact time remains minimal and jump height doesn't decrease. 4. Landing Surface Quality: Use compliant surface (rubber mat, turf) to reduce joint stress while maintaining reactive qualities. 5. Volume Management: Modified depth jumps showed 29.1-48.4% strength gains but require adequate recovery; limit to 2-3 sets of 5 reps maximum per session.

**Scientific Reference:**
PMC 4637913. Current concepts of plyometric exercise and stretch-shortening cycle mechanisms; PMC 11877295. Accentuated eccentric loading in depth jumps vs countermovement jumps - effects on jump performance; Ebben WP (2004). Effects of eccentric phase velocity of plyometric training on vertical jump. Int J Sports Med 25(6):391-398; Sports Performance Bulletin. The drop depth jump for increasing vertical leap and explosive power

**Sports Tags:** Basketball, Volleyball, Track & Field, American Football, Rugby, Soccer, CrossFit, Alpine Skiing, Gymnastics, Ice Hockey, Tennis, General Athletic Performance

**Training_Goal:** Power

**Movement_Pattern:** Jump_Vertical; Eccentric_Emphasis

### EXERCISE 10: SPLIT SQUAT JUMPS (JUMPING LUNGES)

**Primary Muscles:** Quadriceps Femoris (Rectus Femoris, Vastus Lateralis, Vastus Medialis, Vastus Intermedius); Gluteus Maximus

**Secondary Muscles:** Hamstrings (Biceps Femoris, Semitendinosus, Semimembranosus); Gluteus Medius; Adductors; Gastrocnemius; Soleus; Core Stabilizers

**Type:** Compound

**Equipment:** Bodyweight

**Level:** Intermediate

**Body Region:** Lower

**Movement:** Explosive

**Modality:** Plyometric

**Functional Groups:** Quads_Hamstrings_Glutes; Explosive_Power; Unilateral_Strength

**Steps for Beginners:**
1. Start in split stance position with right foot forward, left foot back (3-4 feet apart). 2. Lower into lunge position until front knee reaches 90° and back knee nearly touches ground. 3. Explosively drive through front foot to propel body upward. 4. Switch leg positions in mid-air (scissor motion). 5. Land softly in opposite split stance (left foot forward, right foot back). 6. Immediately descend into next lunge and repeat jump. 7. Continue alternating legs for prescribed repetitions (10-20 total reps).

**Advanced Key Points:**
1. Quadriceps Dominance: Jumping lunges produce greater EMG activity in quadriceps than walking lunges due to explosive eccentric-concentric coupling. 2. Gluteus Maximus Recruitment: Lunges activate glutes more effectively than back squats when standardized; emphasize hip extension during takeoff. 3. Landing Mechanics: Front knee must track over second toe; avoid knee valgus collapse which increases ACL injury risk. 4. Ground Contact Minimization: Reduce contact time to <0.3s between reps to maintain plyometric stimulus and power output. 5. Step Length Variation: Longer step lengths (greater than shoulder-width) increase posterior chain (glutes, hamstrings) activation while reducing quadriceps dominance.

**Scientific Reference:**
PMC 4640053. Dumbbell-carrying position effects on split squats and walking lunges muscle activity; Aniceto RR et al. (2021). Lunges activate gluteus maximus more than back squats when standardized. Isokinetics Exercise Sci 29(2):111-118; PMC 10667687. Effects of step lengths on biomechanics during split squat movement; MDPI (2025). Muscle activation during squat performed in different ranges of motion

**Sports Tags:** Basketball, Soccer, Tennis, American Football, Rugby, Track & Field, CrossFit, Ice Hockey, Alpine Skiing, Volleyball, Fencing, General Fitness

### EXERCISE 11: TUCK JUMPS

**Primary Muscles:** Quadriceps Femoris (all four heads); Gluteus Maximus; Hip Flexors (Iliopsoas, Rectus Femoris)

**Secondary Muscles:** Hamstrings (Biceps Femoris, Semitendinosus, Semimembranosus); Gastrocnemius; Soleus; Rectus Abdominis; Obliques

**Type:** Compound

**Equipment:** Bodyweight

**Level:** Advanced

**Body Region:** Lower

**Movement:** Explosive

**Modality:** Plyometric

**Functional Groups:** Quads_Hamstrings_Glutes; Explosive_Power; Full_Core

**Steps for Beginners:**
1. Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, arms at sides. 2. Lower into quarter squat position (30-45° knee flexion) with arms back. 3. Explosively jump upward driving knees toward chest as high as possible. 4. Simultaneously bring arms forward and grab knees at peak height (optional). 5. Quickly extend legs before landing to prepare for ground contact. 6. Land softly on midfoot with knees bent (20-30° flexion) to absorb impact. 7. Immediately reset and repeat for prescribed repetitions (typically 6-10 reps).

**Advanced Key Points:**
1. Vertical Jump Enhancement: Meta-analysis shows plyometric training improves vertical jump by 4.7% (squat jump) and 8.7% (countermovement jump). 2. Triple Extension Emphasis: Quadriceps contract forcefully to extend knees and generate power; achieve complete ankle-knee-hip extension at takeoff. 3. Hip Flexor Demand: Bringing knees to chest requires rapid hip flexion; iliopsoas works concentrically at high velocity during flight. 4. Landing Assessment: Tuck jumps used as ACL injury risk assessment; avoid knee valgus, asymmetrical landing, or excessive forward trunk lean. 5. Ground Contact Minimization: Minimize contact time (<0.3s) to maintain plyometric effect; pause between reps for pure power development.

**Scientific Reference:**

Tuck jump assessment for reducing ACL injury risk; PMC 12131147. Effects of physical training programs on vertical jump height - meta-analysis; Budapest Int Research (2024). ### EXERCISE 12: SINGLE-LEG HOPS

**Primary Muscles:** Gluteus Medius; Gluteus Maximus; Quadriceps Femoris (Rectus Femoris, Vastus Lateralis, Vastus Medialis)

**Secondary Muscles:** Hamstrings (Biceps Femoris, Semitendinosus, Semimembranosus); Gastrocnemius; Soleus; Hip Stabilizers; Core Stabilizers

**Type:** Compound

**Equipment:** Bodyweight

**Level:** Advanced

**Body Region:** Lower

**Movement:** Explosive

**Modality:** Plyometric

**Functional Groups:** Glutes_Hips; Explosive_Power; Unilateral_Strength; Balance

**Steps for Beginners:**
1. Stand on right leg with slight knee flexion (20-30°), left leg lifted off ground. 2. Lower into quarter squat on right leg maintaining balance. 3. Explosively extend right ankle, knee, and hip to propel body upward or forward. 4. Maintain single-leg position throughout flight phase. 5. Land on same right foot with controlled knee flexion (30-45°) to absorb impact. 6. Stabilize landing for 1-2 seconds maintaining balance and knee alignment. 7. Perform 5-8 hops on right leg, then switch to left leg and repeat.

**Advanced Key Points:**
1. Gluteus Medius Activation Peak: Research shows single-leg sagittal plane hurdle hops produce greatest gluteal activation (100% MVIC) vs other plyometric exercises. 2. ACL Injury Prevention: Single-leg sagittal plane hops highly effective for activating gluteals and hamstrings; important for ACL injury prevention programs. 3. Landing Mechanics Control: Maintain hip-knee-ankle alignment; avoid dynamic knee valgus (inward collapse) which increases injury risk 2.5x. 4. Frontal Plane Stability: Gluteus medius maintains pelvic stability during single-leg stance; trains hip abduction and prevents Trendelenburg gait. 5. Progressive Overload: Start with vertical hops; progress to horizontal bounds, then lateral hops for multidirectional power development.

**Scientific Reference:**

### EXERCISE 13: BOUNDING

**Primary Muscles:** Gluteus Maximus; Hamstrings (Biceps Femoris, Semitendinosus, Semimembranosus); Quadriceps Femoris; Hip Flexors (Iliopsoas)

**Secondary Muscles:** Gastrocnemius; Soleus; Erector Spinae; Core Stabilizers; Hip Adductors

**Type:** Compound

**Equipment:** Bodyweight

**Level:** Advanced

**Body Region:** Lower

**Movement:** Explosive

**Modality:** Plyometric

**Functional Groups:** Quads_Hamstrings_Glutes; Explosive_Power; Horizontal_Power; Sprint_Specific

**Steps for Beginners:**
1. Start in athletic stance with slight forward lean. 2. Push off explosively with right leg, driving right knee and left arm forward. 3. Propel body forward as far as possible emphasizing horizontal distance. 4. Maintain extended flight phase with exaggerated stride length. 5. Land on left foot with controlled knee flexion (20-30°). 6. Immediately push off left leg into next bound without pause. 7. Continue alternating legs for 20-40 meters maintaining rhythm and power.

**Advanced Key Points:**
1. Stride Length Development: Bounding creates adaptations to increase stride length and overall hip extension power for single-leg jumps and sprinting. 2. Horizontal Force Production: Horizontally-oriented plyometrics improve ability to apply force horizontally; essential for acceleration phase of sprint running. 3. Arm Drive Coordination: Single arm drive (speed bounds) mimics sprint action; double arm drive (power bounds) emphasizes maximum distance per bound. 4. Ground Contact Optimization: Short ground contact times (<0.2s) with high step frequency develop elastic tendon properties and reactive strength. 5. Volume and Recovery: Perform 3-4 sets of 20-40m bounds; rest 5x work time (e.g., 12s work = 60s rest) to maintain power output quality.

### EXERCISE 14: HURDLE HOPS

**Primary Muscles:** Gluteus Medius (100% MVIC); Gluteus Maximus; Quadriceps Femoris

**Secondary Muscles:** Hamstrings (Biceps Femoris, Semitendinosus, Semimembranosus - Medial and Lateral heads); Hip Stabilizers; Core Stabilizers; Gastrocnemius

**Type:** Compound

**Equipment:** Hurdles (6-12 inches) or Cones

**Level:** Advanced

**Body Region:** Lower

**Movement:** Explosive

**Modality:** Plyometric

**Functional Groups:** Glutes_Hips; Explosive_Power; Knee_Valgus_Prevention; ACL_Injury_Prevention

**Steps for Beginners:**
1. Set up 4-6 low hurdles (6-12 inches) spaced 2-3 feet apart. 2. Stand on one leg (right) in front of first hurdle with slight knee flexion. 3. Hop forward over hurdle landing on same leg (single-leg variation) or both legs (bilateral variation). 4. Minimize ground contact time (<0.3s) before immediately hopping over next hurdle. 5. Maintain upright posture with core engaged throughout movement. 6. Land with controlled knee flexion (30-45°) ensuring knee tracks over toes. 7. Complete all hurdles, rest, then repeat for 3-4 sets.

**Advanced Key Points:**
1. Gluteal Activation Maximum: Single-leg sagittal plane hurdle hops produce highest gluteus medius activation (100% MVIC) and gluteus maximus activation among plyometric exercises. 2. ACL Injury Prevention Mechanism: High gluteal activity limits knee valgus motion by controlling hip adduction and internal rotation; essential for ACL protection. 3. Landing Phase Control: Core muscles and gluteus medius stabilize body especially during landing; critical for injury prevention during plyometric training. 4. Knee Valgus Assessment: Avoid inward knee collapse during landing; gluteal strengthening reduces valgus motion by providing eccentric resistance to hip adduction. 5. Progressive Hurdle Height: Start 6 inches; progress to 12-18 inches only when landing mechanics remain perfect and ground contact minimal.

**Scientific Reference:**

Hip-focused neuromuscular exercise interventions for dynamic lower extremity valgus - reduces ACL injury risk; PMC 8151944. Impact of strengthening exercises on dynamic knee valgus with poor knee control; Muscle and Motion. Hurdle jump and stick for concentric/eccentric muscle activation

### EXERCISE 15: POGO JUMPS

**Primary Muscles:** Gastrocnemius; Soleus; Quadriceps Femoris (minimal knee bend emphasis)

**Secondary Muscles:** Tibialis Anterior; Hip Flexors; Gluteus Maximus; Core Stabilizers; Achilles Tendon (mechanical)

**Type:** Isolation

**Equipment:** Bodyweight

**Level:** Intermediate

**Body Region:** Lower

**Movement:** Explosive

**Modality:** Plyometric

**Functional Groups:** Calves; Explosive_Power; Ankle_Stiffness; Reactive_Strength

**Steps for Beginners:**
1. Stand with feet hip-width apart, knees slightly bent (10-15° only). 2. Rise onto balls of feet (toes) with minimal knee flexion. 3. Using only ankles and calves, push off ground to jump vertically 2-4 inches. 4. Maintain stiff legs throughout jump (minimal knee bend - <15°). 5. Land on balls of feet and immediately rebound into next jump. 6. Minimize ground contact time to less than 0.2 seconds. 7. Continue rapid-fire jumps for 10-20 repetitions or 10-15 seconds duration.

**Advanced Key Points:**
1. Ankle Stiffness Development: Minimal knee bend forces tendons to store and release elastic energy rapidly; requires high level of ankle/calf stiffness for optimal performance. 2. Reactive Strength Training: Pogo jumps primarily build stretch-shortening cycle tolerance for muscles and tendons; improves neuromuscular control and reactive strength. 3. Force Production Magnitude: Single-leg pogo hops produce 5x bodyweight vertical forces upon ground contact - equivalent to max velocity sprinting forces. 4. Tendon Conditioning: Improves Achilles tendon stiffness which is the ability of structure to rapidly lengthen and shorten under load. 5. Ground Contact Minimization: Contact time <0.2s essential; longer contact shifts emphasis from reactive/elastic to concentric strength reducing plyometric benefit.

**Scientific Reference:**
Garage Strength (2024). Pogo jump for lower limb stiffness, reactive strength, and tendon conditioning; Azide Performance. Pogo jumps enhance ankle stiffness, reactivity, and energy efficiency for sprinting and jumping; Ken Clark research. Single-leg pogo hops result in 5x bodyweight forces - same as max velocity sprinting; Holton PT (2024). Using pogo jumps to introduce plyometrics into running and injury prevention programs

**Sports Tags:** Track & Field (Sprinting), Basketball, Volleyball, Soccer, Running, CrossFit, American Football, Tennis, Jumping Events, Speed Development, Achilles Tendon Rehabilitation

**Training_Goal:** Power

**Modality:** Bodyweight

Push off right foot explosively, stepping laterally to left with left foot. 4. Quickly bring right foot to meet left foot maintaining stance width. 5. Continue shuffling laterally for 10-15 feet keeping hips low and square. 6. Push off left foot to decelerate and reverse direction. 7. Shuffle back to starting position; repeat for desired time (30-60 seconds) or distance.

**Advanced Key Points:**

Frontal Plane Training: Lateral exercises stimulate specific motor patterns and stress abductors/adductors often neglected in sagittal plane movements. 3. Agility Performance Enhancement: Hip activation crucial for agility; lateral shuffles prime hip muscles for quick directional changes and cutting movements. 4. Foot Pattern Discipline: Avoid crossing feet or bringing feet together; maintain constant tension and stance width throughout movement. 5. Speed Progression: Start with slow controlled tempo (2-second steps); progress to explosive tempo for agility and cardiovascular conditioning.

### EXERCISE 17: PLYOMETRIC PUSH-UPS (CLAP PUSH-UPS)

**Primary Muscles:** Pectoralis Major (sternal and clavicular heads); Triceps Brachii; Anterior Deltoid

**Secondary Muscles:** Serratus Anterior; Core Stabilizers (Rectus Abdominis, Obliques); Posterior Deltoid; Trapezius

**Type:** Compound

**Equipment:** Bodyweight

**Level:** Advanced

**Body Region:** Upper

**Movement:** Push

**Modality:** Plyometric

**Functional Groups:** Chest_Pectoralis; Arms_Triceps; Explosive_Power; Full_Core

**Steps for Beginners:**
1. Start in high plank position with hands slightly wider than shoulder-width. 2. Lower chest to ground with controlled 2-second eccentric phase. 3. Explosively push through hands to propel upper body off ground. 4. While airborne, quickly clap hands together once. 5. Return hands to starting position before landing. 6. Land with hands shoulder-width apart, absorbing impact with slight elbow flexion. 7. Immediately descend into next repetition; perform 3-6 reps per set.

**Advanced Key Points:**
1. Peak Force Production: Clap push-ups produce significantly greater peak vertical ground reaction force than all other push-up variations including box drops. 2. Power Output Maximum: Despite similar flight heights to box drops, clap push-ups show greater peak force and elbow displacement indicating highest intensity. 3. Loading Rate Superior: Clap loading rate and propulsion rate significantly greater than box drop variations; most powerful upper body plyometric exercise. 4. Pectoralis Major Activation: EMG shows high activation in sternal and clavicular heads; narrow-base variants increase pec major and triceps recruitment further. 5. Progressive Overload: Begin with elevated push-ups (hands on box); progress to floor clap push-ups; advance to double clap or depth clap push-ups.

**Scientific Reference:**

### EXERCISE 18: MEDICINE BALL SLAMS

**Primary Muscles:** Rectus Abdominis; Latissimus Dorsi; Anterior Deltoid; Triceps Brachii

**Secondary Muscles:** External Obliques; Internal Obliques; Pectoralis Major; Serratus Anterior; Erector Spinae; Hip Flexors; Quadriceps

**Steps for Beginners:**
1. Start on hands and knees in tabletop position. 2. Lift knees 2-3 inches off ground maintaining flat back. 3. Move right hand and left foot forward simultaneously. 4. Follow with left hand and right foot (contralateral pattern). 5. Continue crawling forward maintaining low hip position. 6. Keep core engaged and neutral spine throughout movement. 7. **Advanced Key Points:**
1. Core Activation Superior: Quadrupedal movements create greater core activation than many isolation exercises; TVA acts as stabilizer during entire body movement. 2. Functional Fitness Essence: Maintain neutral spine key for functional movement patterns (walking, squatting, pushing, pulling) and preventing lower-back pain. 5. Increased Resistance: For added challenge, increased need to resist movement from back and hips while limbs perform movement; excellent anterior core exercise.
